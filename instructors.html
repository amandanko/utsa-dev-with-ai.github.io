The delivery model

Students complete each module asynchronously — video, reading, and a short hands-on exercise — before a scheduled in-class session. Class time is not a lecture on the module; it is a studio Q&A grounded in what students bring: their exercise results, their project code, and the specific places an AI tool helped or misled them. The asynchronous layer scales beyond any one section; the studio layer is where your expertise does work no recording can.

A typical pairing for a 15-week semester runs modules 01–03 in the first month (before teams commit to a tool), 04–06 across the mid-semester project build, and 07–08 in the final month as features expand. One studio session per module is enough; many instructors fold it into existing lab time.

Running a studio session (50 min)

  1. Open with artifacts (10 min). Two or three volunteers show their exercise output. Same prompt, different tools, different failures — the comparison is the lesson.
  2. Cluster the questions (10 min). Collect questions submitted with the exercise (a pre-class form works well) and group them live. Patterns across the room beat one-on-one answers.
  3. Work one real problem (20 min). Take a student's actual project issue and work it at the front of the room, narrating your own judgment: what you'd delegate, what you'd verify, when you'd take the wheel. Modeling expert judgment is the highest-value thing in the hour.
  4. Close on the stance (10 min). Return to "you own every line": what would each student need to do before this code earns a merge?

Misconceptions to expect

  • "The tool is the skill." Students arrive equating fluency with one product to competence. The mode taxonomy in Module 01 is the counter; reinforce it by naming modes, not products, in feedback.
  • "It ran, so it works." The single most common gap. Modules 04–05 exist to replace "it ran once" with review and tests; grade the disclosure note and tests, not just the demo.
  • "Disclosure means I'll be penalized." Say explicitly, and early, that honest AI-use notes are never graded down. Otherwise students hide usage and you lose the teaching surface entirely.
  • "More autonomy is more advanced." Strong students often over-delegate. The professional skill is matching autonomy to risk and verifiability — push back on autonomous-mode use for code they can't yet review.

Maintaining the tool layer

Concept modules should need only annual review. Playbooks rot in months by design — and the maintenance plan is the curriculum: Module 08's exercise has students evaluate a tool and draft or update a playbook, the best of which are merged by pull request each semester with the student credited. Budget one hour per semester per playbook for instructor verification; if a playbook can't be verified, mark it stale rather than deleting it. A stale chip is itself a lesson.

Adopting this at your institution

Everything here is open: concept modules under CC BY 4.0, site and exercise code under MIT. Fork the repository, replace the course banner and the integrity-policy callout with your institution's language, and keep or swap the playbooks for the tools your students can access. We ask only for attribution — and we'd love an issue telling us you're using it, plus any survey results you're willing to share for the ongoing SIGCSE work.
